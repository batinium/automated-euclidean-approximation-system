\begin{abstract}
A regular $n$-gon is constructible by ruler and compass iff $n$ is a product of a power of two and distinct Fermat primes.
For non-constructible $n$ (e.g.\ $7, 11, 13$) the exact values $\alpha_n = \cos(2\pi/n)$ live in real subfields of cyclotomic fields of degree greater than a power of two, and therefore cannot be obtained inside the quadratic closure of $\mathbb{Q}$.
This paper investigates how well such non-constructible cosines can nevertheless be approximated by \emph{constructible} numbers: elements of quadratic field towers of bounded depth and bounded coefficient height.

We introduce the \emph{Automated Euclidean Approximation System} (AEAS), which performs a field-first search over quadratic towers and returns explicit nested-square-root expressions built from $\{0,1\}$ using $+,-,\times,\div,\sqrt{\phantom{x}}$.
Empirically, for $n \in \{7,11,13\}$ we observe a clear dependence of the best approximation error $|x - \alpha_n|$ on both the sqrt-depth and a natural ``height'' parameter controlling the granularity of rational coefficients.
Compared to an expression-tree beam search baseline, the field-first search avoids beam collapse at higher depths and often attains strictly better errors under matched resource budgets.
We also identify and characterise a \emph{coefficient resolution floor}: at fixed height, increasing sqrt-depth eventually ceases to improve the best error because the outermost rational coefficients are too coarse.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The classical Gauss--Wantzel theorem completely characterises which regular $n$-gons are constructible with ruler and compass: those with $n = 2^k p_1 \cdots p_m$, where the $p_i$ are distinct Fermat primes.
For non-constructible $n$ such as $7, 11,$ or $13$, the exact value $\alpha_n = \cos(2\pi/n)$ lies in a number field whose degree over $\mathbb{Q}$ is not a power of two, and thus $\alpha_n$ is not a constructible real number.

In contrast, the constructible reals themselves form the smallest field extension of $\mathbb{Q}$ closed under finitely many applications of
\[
  x \mapsto -x, \quad x \mapsto x^{-1}, \quad (x,y) \mapsto x \pm y, \quad (x,y) \mapsto xy, \quad x \mapsto \sqrt{x}
\]
whenever the square root is real.
Every constructible real can therefore be expressed as a nested square-root expression with rational leaves, and can be viewed as an element of a tower of quadratic field extensions of $\mathbb{Q}$.
This perspective naturally leads to a computational question:

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{RQ1 (approximation law).}
\emph{How does the best achievable error $|x - \cos(2\pi/n)|$ depend on the sqrt-depth and coefficient height within quadratic-tower fields?}

\medskip
At the same time, exhaustive enumeration of nested square-root expressions quickly becomes intractable.
Previous work in this project used a beam search over expression trees, which suffers from redundancy and beam collapse at higher depths.
The present paper instead searches \emph{first} in the underlying number fields and only builds expression trees for the most promising candidates, raising a second question:

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{RQ2 (algorithmic comparison).}
\emph{Does a field-first normal-form search over quadratic towers outperform expression-tree beam search for approximating non-constructible $\cos(2\pi/n)$ under matched resource budgets?}

\medskip
Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item We formalise a field-first search over quadratic towers of bounded sqrt-depth and coefficient height, including a fast float pre-filter and a diversity-preserving pruning scheme (Figure~\ref{fig:architecture}).
  \item We implement AEAS, an automated system that searches for constructible approximations to $\cos(2\pi/n)$ for $n \in \{7,11,13\}$ and returns human-readable nested-square-root expressions (Figure~\ref{fig:expr-trees}).
  \item We empirically characterise how the best error scales with depth and height (RQ1), and we show that the field-first search is more robust than an expression-tree beam search baseline at higher depths (RQ2).
  \item We identify a coefficient resolution floor: beyond a certain depth, additional layers of $\sqrt{\phantom{x}}$ cease to improve the error unless the coefficient height budget is increased (Figure~\ref{fig:saturation}).
\end{itemize}

\section{Background}

\subsection{Constructible numbers and quadratic towers}

Classically, a complex number $z$ is constructible by ruler and compass iff it lies in a field extension of $\mathbb{Q}$ whose degree is a power of two.
Equivalently, the constructible reals form the closure of $\mathbb{Q}$ under the operations in the introduction.
Every constructible real can be written as an element of a tower
\[
  \mathbb{Q} \subset \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{a_1}) \subset
  \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{a_1},\sqrt{a_2}) \subset \cdots,
\]
and admits a concrete expression built from rational constants and nested applications of $\sqrt{\phantom{x}}$.

From an algorithmic perspective, one can either search over \emph{syntax} (expression trees) or over \emph{semantics} (coordinates in a tower of quadratic field extensions).
The latter avoids many syntactic symmetries: different trees can represent the same field element.

\subsection{Non-constructible cosines}

For $n\ge 3$, the real number $\alpha_n = \cos(2\pi/n)$ lies in the real subfield of the $n$th cyclotomic field.
When $n$ is not of Gauss--Wantzel form, the degree $[\mathbb{Q}(\alpha_n):\mathbb{Q}]$ is not a power of two, so $\alpha_n$ is not constructible.
We focus on the smallest such cases $n \in \{7,11,13\}$.
They already exhibit the phenomenon that motivates this work: high-quality approximants can live at moderate tower depth but require sufficiently fine coefficient resolution.

\section{Methods: the AEAS system}

\subsection{Expression representation}

Internally, AEAS represents candidates as immutable expression trees.
Each node is an application of one of the operations
\[
  \mathrm{CONST},\ \mathrm{ADD},\ \mathrm{SUB},\ \mathrm{MUL},\ \mathrm{DIV},\ \mathrm{SQRT},
\]
with rational constants at the leaves.
The tree class caches its sqrt-depth (maximum nesting of $\sqrt{\phantom{x}}$), total node count, and a canonical parenthesised string representation.
Structural hashing and equality ensure that two trees with the same shape and leaves compare equal and share a hash.

A separate canonicalisation pass performs constant folding, eliminates algebraic identities (e.g.\ $x+0 \mapsto x$, $\sqrt{1}\mapsto 1$), and sorts children of commutative operations to normalise expressions such as $a+b$ and $b+a$.
All search algorithms use this canonical form, so duplicates are removed at the level of trees, not just field elements.

\subsection{Field-first search over quadratic towers}

The core of AEAS is a field-first search over quadratic towers, illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:architecture}.
At a fixed maximum sqrt-depth $d$ and coefficient height $H$, the search proceeds depth-wise:

\paragraph{Depth 0 (rationals).}
We enumerate reduced fractions $A = p/q$ with $1 \le q \le H$ and value in a bounded interval, together with one or two best-approximation rationals obtained from a continued-fraction style heuristic.
Each candidate is canonicalised into an expression tree and evaluated at $80$ decimal digits of precision using \texttt{mpmath}.

\paragraph{Depth 1 (quadratic surds).}
For each squarefree radicand $m \le 30$ and each rational $A$ from the depth-0 pool, we consider expressions of the form
\[
  x = A + B\sqrt{m}
\]
where $B$ ranges over rationals of height at most $H$.
Rather than enumerating all such $B$ values, we compute a real-valued ``best'' coefficient $(\alpha_n - A)/\sqrt{m}$ and then search a small rational neighbourhood around it.
Millions of such tuples $(A,B,m)$ are scored in fast float arithmetic, keeping only the top few times the beam width in a bounded heap.
Expression trees are materialised and evaluated with \texttt{mpmath} only for these survivors.

\paragraph{Depth $\bm{\ge 2}$ (nested towers).}
Deeper tower levels have the form
\[
  x = P + Q\sqrt{\text{inner}_{d-1}},
\]
where $\text{inner}_{d-1}$ is a positive-valued depth-$(d-1)$ expression drawn from the previous level.
We reuse the same guided coefficient search strategy over $(P,Q)$, plus a biquadratic refinement step that adds small corrections of the form $C\sqrt{n}$ on top of the best surds.
At each level we maintain a global pool of candidates across all sqrt-depths up to $d$, pruned by a deterministic diversity-aware beam that reserves slots for each sqrt-depth value.

\paragraph{Evaluation and pruning.}
All candidates are evaluated at $80$ decimal digits of precision.
Invalid expressions (division by zero, negative square roots, non-finite results) are rejected.
The pool is sorted by a stable key \((\text{error}, \text{node count}, \text{string})\), ensuring reproducible ranking.
The search returns, for each depth, the list of top-$K$ candidates with their errors and expression trees.

\subsection{Expression-tree beam search baseline}

For comparison, we implement a beam search over expression trees.
Starting from a small seed set of rational constants, the beam expands by applying binary operations and $\sqrt{\phantom{x}}$ up to a maximum node count and sqrt-depth.
At each round a global beam of fixed width is retained according to the same sort key as above.
This baseline is simpler conceptually but suffers from heavy syntactic redundancy and can collapse at higher depths when many syntactically different trees map to similar numeric values.

\section{Experimental setup}

We target $n \in \{7,11,13\}$ and approximate $\alpha_n = \cos(2\pi/n)$ at $80$ digits of precision.
Our experiments fall into three families:

\paragraph{Field vs beam (RQ2).}
We compare the field-first search and the expression-tree beam search under matched resource budgets: sqrt-depth up to $3$, beam width $2000$, and a coefficient height budget $H=20$ on the field-first side.
For each $(n,d)$ we record the best error, node count, sqrt-depth, and runtime.

\paragraph{Height scaling at fixed depth (RQ1).}
We study how the best error at depth $d \in \{1,2\}$ scales with the height budget $H \in \{8,12,16,24,32,48\}$.
For each $(n,d,H)$ we run the field-first search with fixed radicand bound and beam width, then fit a simple log--log trend to $\log_{10}(\text{error})$ versus $\log_{10}(H)$.

\paragraph{Depth scaling at fixed height (RQ1/RQ2).}
Fixing a moderately large height budget $H=32$, we vary the maximum sqrt-depth $D \in \{0,1,2,3,4\}$ and measure the best error per actual depth level.
This reveals both improvement ratios between successive depths and a depth beyond which additional nesting no longer helps.
A control experiment at $H=64$ shows how raising the coefficient resolution shifts this saturation point.

All experiments are deterministic given the configuration, and each run writes a machine-readable summary to the \texttt{results/} directory.
Aggregate tables and figures are produced by the analysis scripts in \texttt{scripts/analyse\_scaling.py} and \texttt{scripts/visualize\_search.py}.

To test whether this stagnation is an algorithmic artifact or a structural limitation, we repeat the depth-scaling experiment at $H=64$ for $n\in\{7,11,13\}$.
Figure~\ref{fig:saturation} compares the best errors at $H=32$ and $H=64$.
For $n=7$, increasing $H$ from $32$ to $64$ improves the depth-2 error by a factor of roughly $29$ (from $1.25\times10^{-8}$ to $4.36\times10^{-10}$) and shifts the saturation point from depth~3 to depth~2.
For $n=11$, the depth-0 error improves by more than two orders of magnitude at $H=64$.
In all cases, there exists a depth beyond which further nesting cannot improve the error at fixed height, and increasing the height can move this saturation point.
This is precisely the behaviour one would expect from a coefficient resolution floor.

\section{Discussion}

Our experiments suggest a clear answer to RQ1: within quadratic towers of bounded depth and coefficient height, the best achievable error obeys a mixed law in which both parameters matter.
At small heights, depth increases can yield dramatic improvements---for instance, at $H=32$ for $n=7$ the error drops from $8.68\times10^{-7}$ at depth~0 to $2.51\times10^{-10}$ at depth~3.
But for each fixed height there is a depth at which the error saturates, beyond which increasing depth alone does not help.
Raising the height budget lowers this floor and can shift the saturation depth.

For RQ2, the field-first search provides a more robust and interpretable exploration of the space of constructible numbers.
By working in normal form and using a guided coefficient search, it avoids the syntactic redundancy of expression trees and maintains diversity across different sqrt-depth levels.
The beam baseline is easier to implement but more fragile: it can collapse at higher depths, with the beam filled by near-duplicates that crowd out promising but temporarily suboptimal candidates.
The quantitative results in Table~\ref{tab:field-vs-beam} and Figures~\ref{fig:depth-scaling}--\ref{fig:tower-layers} make this contrast explicit.

More broadly, we view AEAS as a proof-of-concept for using automated search over algebraic structures to test approximation hypotheses.
The same framework could be applied to other non-constructible targets, higher-degree towers, or alternative cost models (e.g.\ penalising expression size or radical complexity).

\section{Conclusion and future work}

We presented AEAS, an automated system that searches quadratic towers of bounded depth and coefficient height for constructible approximations to non-constructible cosines.
Our field-first search algorithm outperforms a tree-based beam baseline at higher depths, and reveals a coefficient resolution floor that explains the observed saturation with respect to depth.
These findings highlight the importance of viewing constructible approximations through the lens of field extensions rather than raw syntax.

Future work includes extending the search to higher-degree algebraic towers, exploring additional targets beyond $\cos(2\pi/n)$, and integrating structural filters such as Chebyshev residuals to further reduce the cost of high-precision evaluation.
We also hope that AEAS-style systems can serve as experimental testbeds for conjectures about algebraic approximation and constructive geometry.
