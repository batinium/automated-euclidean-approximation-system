\begin{abstract}
This paper presents the \emph{Automated Euclidean Approximation System} (AEAS), a reproducible search system for constructible-number approximations to non-constructible targets of the form $\alpha_n=\cos(2\pi/n)$.
The mathematical setting is classical (constructible reals via quadratic towers), but the main contribution is algorithmic: AEAS replaces syntax-first expression enumeration with a field-first normal-form search, combining fast float pre-filtering, selective tree materialisation, high-precision verification, and deterministic diversity-preserving pruning.

We evaluate on $n\in\{7,11,13\}$ under controlled depth/height budgets and provide baseline and robustness comparisons against expression-tree beam search.
Results show clear depth-height tradeoffs, strong practical performance of the field-first pipeline in baseline settings, and a depth-saturation behaviour consistent with coefficient-resolution limits.
We also report parameter-sensitivity sweeps for beam width, radicand bound, precision, and beam node budgets, enabling a transparent justification of default settings and computational tradeoffs.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The Gauss--Wantzel theorem implies that many regular polygons are not ruler-and-compass constructible.
For such cases (e.g.\ $n\in\{7,11,13\}$), $\alpha_n=\cos(2\pi/n)$ is non-constructible, yet can still be approximated by constructible numbers.

The constructible reals form the smallest field extension of $\mathbb{Q}$ closed under finitely many applications of
\[
  x \mapsto -x, \quad x \mapsto x^{-1}, \quad (x,y) \mapsto x \pm y, \quad (x,y) \mapsto xy, \quad x \mapsto \sqrt{x}
\]
whenever the square root is real.
Every constructible real can therefore be represented either syntactically (expression trees) or semantically (coordinates in quadratic towers).
The central systems question is how to search this space efficiently and reproducibly.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{RQ1 (performance law).}
\emph{How does the best achievable error $|x - \cos(2\pi/n)|$ depend on the sqrt-depth and coefficient height within quadratic-tower fields?}

\medskip
Exhaustive tree enumeration becomes intractable quickly due to combinatorial redundancy.
Prior project versions used beam search over expression trees; here we redesign the search pipeline to operate field-first and materialise trees only for top survivors, raising a second question:

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{RQ2 (algorithmic comparison).}
\emph{How does a field-first normal-form search over quadratic towers compare with expression-tree beam search for approximating non-constructible $\cos(2\pi/n)$ under comparable practical budgets?}

\medskip
Our contributions are:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Search-system design.} We formalise a field-first search over quadratic towers with two-stage candidate handling (float pre-filter $\rightarrow$ selective symbolic materialisation) and deterministic diversity-preserving pruning (Figure~\ref{fig:architecture}).
  \item \textbf{Implementation artifact.} We implement AEAS as a reproducible pipeline that emits machine-readable run records, summaries, and visual diagnostics.
  \item \textbf{Empirical evaluation.} We characterise depth-height performance scaling and compare against a beam-search baseline under comparable practical budgets.
  \item \textbf{Sensitivity analysis.} We include compact sweeps for beam width, radicand bound, precision, and beam node budgets to document default-setting tradeoffs.
\end{itemize}

\section{Background}

\subsection{Constructible numbers and quadratic towers}

Classically, a complex number $z$ is constructible by ruler and compass iff it lies in a field extension of $\mathbb{Q}$ whose degree is a power of two.
Equivalently, the constructible reals form the closure of $\mathbb{Q}$ under the operations in the introduction.
Every constructible real can be written as an element of a tower
\[
  \mathbb{Q} \subset \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{a_1}) \subset
  \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{a_1},\sqrt{a_2}) \subset \cdots,
\]
and admits a concrete expression built from rational constants and nested applications of $\sqrt{\phantom{x}}$.

From an algorithmic perspective, one can either search over \emph{syntax} (expression trees) or over \emph{semantics} (coordinates in a tower of quadratic field extensions).
The latter avoids many syntactic symmetries: different trees can represent the same field element.

\subsection{Non-constructible cosines}

For $n\ge 3$, the real number $\alpha_n = \cos(2\pi/n)$ lies in the real subfield of the $n$th cyclotomic field.
When $n$ is not of Gauss--Wantzel form, the degree $[\mathbb{Q}(\alpha_n):\mathbb{Q}]$ is not a power of two, so $\alpha_n$ is not constructible.
We focus on the smallest such cases $n \in \{7,11,13\}$.
They already exhibit the phenomenon that motivates this work: high-quality approximants can live at moderate tower depth but require sufficiently fine coefficient resolution.

\section{System and Algorithms}

\subsection{Expression representation}

Internally, AEAS represents candidates as immutable expression trees.
Each node is an application of one of the operations
\[
  \mathrm{CONST},\ \mathrm{ADD},\ \mathrm{SUB},\ \mathrm{MUL},\ \mathrm{DIV},\ \mathrm{SQRT},
\]
with rational constants at the leaves.
The tree class caches its sqrt-depth (maximum nesting of $\sqrt{\phantom{x}}$), total node count, and a canonical parenthesised string representation.
Structural hashing and equality ensure that two trees with the same shape and leaves compare equal and share a hash.

A separate canonicalisation pass performs constant folding, eliminates algebraic identities (e.g.\ $x+0 \mapsto x$, $\sqrt{1}\mapsto 1$), and sorts children of commutative operations to normalise expressions such as $a+b$ and $b+a$.
All search algorithms use this canonical form, so duplicates are removed at the level of trees, not just field elements.

\subsection{Field-first search over quadratic towers}

The core of AEAS is a field-first search over quadratic towers, illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:architecture}.
At a fixed maximum sqrt-depth $d$ and coefficient height $H$, the search proceeds depth-wise:

\paragraph{Depth 0 (rationals).}
We enumerate reduced fractions $A = p/q$ with $1 \le q \le H$ and value in a bounded interval, together with one or two best-approximation rationals obtained from a continued-fraction style heuristic.
Each candidate is canonicalised into an expression tree and evaluated at $80$ decimal digits of precision using \texttt{mpmath}.

\paragraph{Depth 1 (quadratic surds).}
For each squarefree radicand $m \le 30$ and each rational $A$ from the depth-0 pool, we consider expressions of the form
\[
  x = A + B\sqrt{m}
\]
where $B$ ranges over rationals of height at most $H$.
Rather than enumerating all such $B$ values, we compute a real-valued ``best'' coefficient $(\alpha_n - A)/\sqrt{m}$ and then search a small rational neighbourhood around it.
Millions of such tuples $(A,B,m)$ are scored in fast float arithmetic, keeping only the top few times the beam width in a bounded heap.
Expression trees are materialised and evaluated with \texttt{mpmath} only for these survivors.

\paragraph{Depth $\bm{\ge 2}$ (nested towers).}
Deeper tower levels have the form
\[
  x = P + Q\sqrt{\text{inner}_{d-1}},
\]
where $\text{inner}_{d-1}$ is a positive-valued depth-$(d-1)$ expression drawn from the previous level.
We reuse the same guided coefficient search strategy over $(P,Q)$, plus a biquadratic refinement step that adds small corrections of the form $C\sqrt{n}$ on top of the best surds.
At each level we maintain a global pool of candidates across all sqrt-depths up to $d$, pruned by a deterministic diversity-aware beam that reserves slots for each sqrt-depth value.
In the current implementation, the denominator search for the outer coefficient at depth $\ge 2$ is capped (\texttt{q\_height} $\le 14$) for compute control; this matters when interpreting depth-vs-height saturation.

\paragraph{Evaluation and pruning.}
All candidates are evaluated at $80$ decimal digits of precision.
Invalid expressions (division by zero, negative square roots, non-finite results) are rejected.
The pool is sorted by a stable key \((\text{error}, \text{node count}, \text{string})\), ensuring reproducible ranking.
The search returns, for each depth, the list of top-$K$ candidates with their errors and expression trees.

\subsection{Algorithmic details and computational profile}

The pseudocode below summarises the implemented field-first pipeline.
The core systems idea is to keep expensive symbolic materialisation and high-precision evaluation off the critical path by scoring very large candidate sets in float arithmetic first, then promoting only top survivors.

\paragraph{Implementation-aligned pseudocode (field-first search).}
\begin{quote}\small
\begin{verbatim}
Input: alpha_n, max_depth, max_height, max_radicand, beam_width, dps
squarefrees <- squarefree_up_to(max_radicand)
rationals   <- bounded_rationals(max_height)
pool <- register(depth0 rationals + best rational approximants)

for d in 1..max_depth:
  if d == 1:
    heap_size <- 5 * beam_width
    for m in squarefrees:
      for A in rationals:
        B* <- (alpha_n - A) / sqrt(m)
        for B in local_rational_neighborhood(B*):
          score (A,B,m) in float and keep top heap_size in bounded heap
    materialize trees only for heap survivors
    evaluate survivors with mpmath(dps), reject invalid, register
    biquadratic refine top depth-1 surds with + C*sqrt(r)
  else:
    inner <- positive depth-(d-1) trees, capped to top 500
    q_h   <- min(max_height, 14)   # current implementation cap
    float-score P + Q*sqrt(inner) neighborhood tuples with bounded heap
    materialize/evaluate top survivors and register
    biquadratic refine top depth-d candidates

  deterministic prune by sort key (error, node_count, to_str)
  with per-sqrt-depth reservation; snapshot best list for depth d

return best candidates per depth
\end{verbatim}
\end{quote}

\paragraph{Complexity drivers.}
Table~\ref{tab:complexity} reports practical search-size drivers.
Ignoring constants, depth-1 float scoring scales roughly as
\[
\mathcal{O}\!\left(|\mathcal{M}| \cdot |\mathcal{A}| \cdot H \cdot w_B\right),
\]
where $|\mathcal{M}|$ is the number of squarefree radicands, $|\mathcal{A}|$ is the depth-0 rational pool size, $H$ is the denominator bound, and $w_B$ is the local numerator window width.
Depth-$d\ge 2$ scoring similarly scales with the number of retained inner candidates.
In contrast, symbolic/materialised candidates are capped by beam-related constants, keeping memory usage near-linear in \texttt{beam\_width}.

\paragraph{Implementation choices.}
The implementation uses bounded heaps for float pre-filter stages (instead of storing all raw tuples), structural canonicalisation-before-insert for deterministic deduplication, and cached \texttt{mpmath} evaluation with guard digits (\texttt{dps}+15).
Invalid evaluations are rejected early and never propagated.
These implementation choices are consistent with the runtime/memory patterns observed in the reported sweeps.

\subsection{Expression-tree beam search baseline}

For comparison, we implement a beam search over expression trees.
Starting from a small seed set of rational constants, the beam expands by applying binary operations and $\sqrt{\phantom{x}}$ up to a maximum node count and sqrt-depth.
At each round a global beam of fixed width is retained according to the same sort key as above.
This baseline is simpler conceptually but suffers from heavy syntactic redundancy and can collapse at higher depths when many syntactically different trees map to similar numeric values.

\section{Experimental setup}

We target $n \in \{7,11,13\}$ and approximate $\alpha_n = \cos(2\pi/n)$ at $80$ digits of precision.
Our experiments fall into three families:

\paragraph{Field vs beam (RQ2).}
We compare the field-first search and the expression-tree beam search under comparable practical budgets: sqrt-depth up to $3$ and beam width $2000$, with method-specific capacity controls (field uses coefficient-height/radicand bounds and enforces a larger node budget internally; beam uses explicit node-count bounds).
For each $(n,d)$ we record the best error, node count, sqrt-depth, and runtime.

\paragraph{Height scaling at fixed depth (RQ1).}
We study how the best error at depth $d \in \{1,2\}$ scales with the height budget $H \in \{8,12,16,24,32,48\}$.
For each $(n,d,H)$ we run the field-first search with fixed radicand bound and beam width, then fit a simple log--log trend to $\log_{10}(\text{error})$ versus $\log_{10}(H)$.

\paragraph{Depth scaling at fixed height (RQ1/RQ2).}
Fixing a moderately large height budget $H=32$, we vary the maximum sqrt-depth $D \in \{0,1,2,3,4\}$ and measure the best error per actual depth level.
This reveals both improvement ratios between successive depths and a depth beyond which additional nesting no longer helps.
A control experiment at $H=64$ shows how raising the coefficient resolution shifts this saturation point.

All experiments are deterministic given the configuration, and each run writes a machine-readable summary to the \texttt{results/} directory.
Aggregate tables and figures are produced by the analysis scripts in \texttt{scripts/analyse\_scaling.py} and \texttt{scripts/visualize\_search.py}.
We additionally ran compact parameter-sensitivity sweeps for default-setting: field beam-width (\(1000,2000,5000\)), field radicand bound (\(20,30,40\)), field precision (\(\texttt{dps}=60,80,120\)), and beam node budgets (\(15,25,35\)).
These sweeps show that \texttt{beam\_width=2000} and \texttt{dps=80} are a practical quality/runtime tradeoff for this benchmark set, and that beam-baseline quality changes strongly with \texttt{max\_nodes}.

\section{Discussion}

For RQ1, results indicate a mixed depth-height performance law: both parameters matter, and gains are regime-dependent rather than monotone in depth alone.
At small heights, depth increases can yield dramatic improvements---for instance, at $H=32$ for $n=7$ the error drops from $8.68\times10^{-7}$ at depth~0 to $2.51\times10^{-10}$ at depth~3.
But for each fixed height there is a depth at which the error saturates, beyond which increasing depth alone does not help.
Raising the height budget lowers this floor and can shift the saturation depth.

For RQ2, the baseline table shows that field-first is lower-error in most reported baseline cells, but not all.
By working in normal form and using a guided coefficient search, it avoids the syntactic redundancy of expression trees and maintains diversity across different sqrt-depth levels.
The beam baseline is easier to implement but more fragile: it can collapse at higher depths, with the beam filled by near-duplicates that crowd out promising but temporarily suboptimal candidates.
Table~\ref{tab:field-vs-beam} and Figures~\ref{fig:depth-scaling}--\ref{fig:tower-layers} show this baseline contrast, while the best-of table shows that ranking can change under stronger beam budgets.

\subsection{Limitations}

Our analysis has four primary limitations.
First, field-vs-beam comparisons use method-specific capacity controls rather than perfectly matched search spaces.
Second, depth-scaling curves are pooled minima across multiple runs, which can overstate monotonic behaviour relative to a single-run trajectory.
Third, the current field implementation caps the depth-$\ge2$ outer-coefficient denominator search (\texttt{q\_height} $\le 14$), which can affect observed saturation behaviour.
Fourth, results are deterministic but do not include uncertainty intervals or stochastic robustness statistics.

From a systems perspective, AEAS is a proof-of-concept for search over algebraic structures with explicit reproducibility and determinism constraints.
The framework can be extended to other non-constructible targets, higher-degree towers, and alternative objective functions (e.g.\ size-penalised expressions).

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}

We presented AEAS as a computational search system for constructible approximation in quadratic towers.
In the reported baseline settings, field-first is frequently lower-error than the tree-based beam baseline, and the sensitivity runs identify regimes where that ordering narrows or reverses.
The main technical takeaway is that semantic search-space design (field-first normal forms) materially changes practical search efficiency compared with raw syntax-first enumeration.

Future work includes extending to higher-degree towers, adding explicit compute-budget-matched comparisons, integrating Chebyshev-style structural filters, and releasing benchmark-style evaluation suites for algebraic approximation search.
